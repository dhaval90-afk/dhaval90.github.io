The Art of the Follow-Up — Why Persistence Beats Perfection

October 2024 · 4 min read

There's a deal I almost lost that I think about often. A prospect I'd been chasing for three months went completely silent after what I thought was a genuinely great second meeting. No replies to emails. Voicemail every time. My manager told me to move on. I sent one last message — not a pitch, just a short note about something I'd read that I genuinely thought would help them. Three days later, they signed.

That deal taught me everything I know about follow-up. The difference between a salesperson who gives up and one who closes isn't talent or product quality. It's the willingness to keep showing up — intelligently, consistently and without desperation.

Silence from a prospect is not a no. It's usually just noise. The world is loud and your email is one of two hundred they received that day.

Why Most Follow-Ups Fail

The problem isn't that salespeople don't follow up — it's that they follow up badly. The classic "just checking in" email is possibly the least effective sentence in the history of B2B sales. It adds no value, signals no preparation and gives the prospect absolutely no reason to respond.

Bad follow-up feels like chasing. Good follow-up feels like serving. The moment you reframe your follow-up as an opportunity to bring value rather than extract a response, everything changes.

The Four Types of Follow-Up That Actually Work

The Value Add Share a relevant article, a stat, a case study or an insight that connects directly to a problem they mentioned. No pitch attached. Just genuine usefulness. This is the follow-up that builds trust over time.
The Pattern Interrupt After two or three ignored emails, change the channel entirely. Call instead of email. Send a voice note on LinkedIn. A handwritten note if the deal is big enough. The unexpected medium gets the attention the expected one couldn't.
The Honest Check-In "I want to be respectful of your time — if priorities have shifted and this isn't the right moment, just say the word and I'll follow up in Q3." Giving someone permission to say no often makes them say yes. It removes the pressure and signals that you're a professional, not a pest.
The Trigger Follow-Up When something changes in their world — a news story, a funding announcement, a LinkedIn post they made — reach out in the context of that event. "I saw your company just announced expansion into Southeast Asia — that's exactly the territory challenge we were discussing. Worth a quick call?"

Timing Matters More Than You Think

Most salespeople follow up too quickly at first and then give up too soon. The sweet spot I've found is a cadence that spaces out over time — a day after the meeting, then three days, then a week, then two weeks. Each touchpoint brings something new. None of them repeat the same ask.

And the timing of day matters too. Tuesday to Thursday mornings between 8am and 10am consistently outperform everything else in my experience across both Indian and North American markets. People are at their desks, their inbox is manageable and they haven't yet been pulled into the chaos of the day.

The number that changed how I think about follow-up

Most deals close after the fifth to eighth touchpoint. Most salespeople give up after the second. That gap — between where salespeople stop and where deals actually close — is where consistent follow-up lives. That's your opportunity.

Persistence Without Desperation

There's a fine line between persistence and pestering and it comes down to one thing: are you following up for them or for you? If every message is about moving your pipeline forward, prospects feel it. If every message is genuinely trying to help them make a good decision, they feel that too.

The best follow-up I've ever sent wasn't a pitch. It was a two-line message that said I'd been thinking about a challenge they'd mentioned and had an idea worth five minutes of their time. No deck attached. No calendar link. Just a human reaching out to another human with something worth saying.

That's the art of it. Not perfect emails. Not automated sequences. Just genuine, consistent, valuable presence — until the timing is right for them.
