Case Study · New Optical Palace

Turning a High-Churn Account Base Into Loyal Partners

New Optical Palace · Canada · 2018–2025

The Situation

When I stepped into the Sales and Customer Success Manager role at New Optical Palace in Canada, the business had a solid product offering and a growing client base — but retention was a persistent problem. Accounts were cycling in and out, satisfaction scores were inconsistent, and the relationship between the company and its B2B clients felt transactional rather than strategic.

The challenge was clear: convert a revolving door of accounts into a stable, loyal and growing portfolio.

The Challenge

Churn in B2B relationships rarely happens suddenly. It builds slowly — through unresolved issues, unmet expectations and the gradual feeling on the client's side that they're just another account number. By the time a client is ready to leave, the relationship has usually been eroding for months.

The difficulty was identifying where the erosion was happening and intervening before it became irreversible — across 35+ accounts simultaneously, each at a different stage of the relationship and with different expectations.

The Approach

The first thing I did was get closer to the data. I worked with our CRM to build a clearer picture of which accounts were showing early warning signs — reduced engagement, slower response times, unresolved support tickets. These became my priority list, not the accounts that were loudest, but the ones that were quietly pulling away.

For each at-risk account, I scheduled a no-agenda call — not to sell, not to check in on a renewal, but to genuinely understand what their experience had been and where we had fallen short. Those conversations were uncomfortable at times, but they were the ones that saved relationships.

I also worked cross-functionally with product, operations and support teams to resolve issues faster. I made it a personal commitment that 90% of customer issues would be resolved without the client having to follow up twice. That single change had an outsized impact on how clients felt about the partnership.

On the proactive side, I introduced regular quarterly business reviews with key accounts — structured conversations about their goals, not ours. This shifted the dynamic from reactive to strategic and gave clients a reason to stay invested in the relationship.

What I Learned

Retention is a sales skill, not just a customer success skill. The same listening, discovery and relationship-building that wins new business is what keeps existing business. The salespeople who treat the post-sale relationship as someone else's problem are leaving their most valuable pipeline — renewals and expansions — on the table.

The clients who stay aren't the ones who never had problems. They're the ones whose problems were handled so well that it actually deepened their trust in you.

The key takeaway

Churn is almost always a relationship problem before it becomes a commercial one. The earlier you catch it and the more genuinely you engage with it, the easier it is to turn around. Don't wait for the renewal conversation to find out how a client really feels.
