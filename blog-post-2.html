Why Most Salespeople Lose Deals Before the First Meeting

June 2024 · 4 min read

Here's a uncomfortable truth I had to learn early in my career: by the time you walk into a first meeting, the deal is already half won or half lost. Not because of your pitch. Not because of your pricing. But because of everything you did — or didn't do — before you walked through that door.

I've sat across from hundreds of prospects over the past 8 years. And the pattern I keep seeing isn't that salespeople fail during the meeting. It's that they show up completely unprepared for the conversation the prospect actually wants to have.

Preparation isn't about knowing your product inside out. It's about knowing your prospect's world better than they expect you to.

The Preparation Gap

Most salespeople spend 80% of their pre-meeting time reviewing their own materials — their deck, their demo script, their pricing tiers. They rehearse answers to objections they assume will come up. They polish their opening line.

Meanwhile the prospect has spent zero time thinking about your product. They're thinking about their quarterly targets, their team's morale, the competitor breathing down their neck, and the three other meetings they have that day. When you walk in talking about features and integrations, you're speaking a completely different language to the one running in their head.

The salespeople who win consistently flip this ratio. They spend the majority of their preparation time researching the prospect's world — and only a fraction reviewing their own pitch.

What Good Preparation Actually Looks Like

Before any significant meeting, I run through what I call my Pre-Meeting Five. It takes about 20 minutes and it has fundamentally changed the quality of my first conversations.

Their recent news. Check the company's LinkedIn page, press releases and Google News in the last 90 days. A funding round, a new product launch, a leadership change — these are golden conversation openers that show you pay attention.

Their market pressure. What is the broader industry dealing with right now? If I'm meeting a logistics company, I want to know what's happening in the supply chain space. If it's a fintech, I want to understand the regulatory climate. Context is currency.

The person, not just the role. Look at their LinkedIn activity. What have they posted or commented on recently? What did their career path look like? People buy from people who understand them — and a small personal insight goes a long way.

Their likely pain. Based on their role and company stage, what problems are they almost certainly dealing with? A VP of Sales at a 200-person Series B company has very different headaches to one at a 2,000-person enterprise. Know which conversation you're walking into.

One smart question. Prepare one question so specific and so well-researched that it makes them sit up slightly. Not "what are your goals this year?" but "I noticed you recently expanded into the US market — how has that changed the way your team manages pipeline across time zones?"

The rule I live by

Never ask a prospect something you could have found out yourself in 10 minutes of research. Every lazy question costs you credibility. Every sharp, informed question earns it.

The First Five Minutes Are Everything

When you walk in prepared this way, something shifts. Instead of starting with "let me tell you about us," you can start with "I've been looking at what you're doing in X space and I had a few thoughts — but first I'd love to understand how you're thinking about Y." That opener alone puts you in a different category to every other salesperson they've met that week.

Prospects decide within the first five minutes whether this meeting is going to be worth their time. Preparation is what earns you the right to the next 55.

The deal isn't lost in the negotiation. It isn't lost in the demo. It's lost in the 24 hours before the meeting, when someone decided their pitch mattered more than their prospect's reality. Don't be that person.
