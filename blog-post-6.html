The Trust Ladder — How Relationships Progress from Cold to Champion

November 2025 · 4 min read

Every deal you've ever closed started with a stranger. Someone who didn't know you, didn't trust you and had no particular reason to give you their time. And somehow, through a series of interactions — some planned, some accidental — that stranger became an advocate who championed your solution internally and maybe even referred you to someone else.

That journey doesn't happen randomly. It follows a pattern I've come to think of as the Trust Ladder — a progression of relationship stages, each with its own rules, its own risks and its own signal that tells you it's time to move up.

Why Most Salespeople Skip Rungs

The most common mistake I see is treating a Curious prospect like a Believer. You get one good discovery call, the prospect asks sharp questions and seems engaged — and suddenly you're sending a full proposal, a pricing deck and a calendar link for a closing call. You've skipped two rungs and the relationship can't hold the weight.

Every rung requires something specific from you before the prospect is ready to climb. Skipping rungs doesn't accelerate the deal — it destabilises it. The prospect feels rushed, pulls back and you find yourself wondering what went wrong after what seemed like a promising start.

What Each Rung Actually Requires

Cold → Aware requires a reason to pay attention. Not a pitch — a pattern interrupt. Something specific enough to make them feel seen rather than targeted. A reference to their recent news, a shared connection, a genuinely relevant insight. The goal is one reply, not a meeting.

Aware → Curious requires value before ask. Every touchpoint at this stage should give something — a useful article, a relevant case study, a question that makes them think. You are building a reason to be worth their time before you ask for any of it.

Curious → Believer requires a great discovery conversation. One where they feel heard, where you surface the real problem beneath the stated problem and where you demonstrate that you understand their world well enough to have an opinion on it. This is where sales skill matters most.

Believer → Ally requires consistency and follow-through. Do what you say you'll do, when you say you'll do it. Share information that helps them navigate their own organisation. Make it easy for them to say yes internally by giving them the language and the evidence they need to convince others.

Ally → Champion requires that you make them look good. When your solution delivers — when the outcomes are real and visible — a great ally becomes a champion almost automatically. Your job at this stage is to make sure the value is visible and attributed to their decision to bring you in.

You don't close a champion. You earn one — slowly, consistently and by caring about their success more than your commission.

The question to ask at every stage

Before your next touchpoint with any prospect, ask yourself: which rung are they on right now — and what does this specific interaction need to do to help them feel ready to climb to the next one? That single question will change the quality of every conversation you have.

The Trust Ladder isn't a shortcut. It's a map. And like any map, it's most useful when you know exactly where you are before you decide where to go next.
