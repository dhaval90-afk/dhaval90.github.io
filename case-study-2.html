Case Study · Dokk Clinics

From Idea to Revenue — Co-Founding Dokk Clinics and Hitting $500K in Year One

Dokk Clinics · India · 2017–2020

The Situation

In 2017, diabetes management in India was almost entirely reactive — patients came in when something went wrong, not before. CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitoring) technology existed but had almost no adoption outside of specialist hospitals. We saw an opportunity to change that by bringing remote, CGM-based monitoring directly to patients through a clinic model that combined technology with personalised care.

Co-founding Dokk Clinics meant building everything simultaneously — the service, the partnerships, the operations and the revenue engine — with no brand, no track record and no existing customer base to lean on.

The Challenge

The challenge wasn't just selling a service. It was selling a behaviour change. Convincing patients and doctors alike to move from episodic clinic visits to continuous remote monitoring required overcoming deeply ingrained habits and a general unfamiliarity with what CGM could actually do in a real-world setting.

On the business side, we had to build doctor and institutional partnerships from scratch, create operational systems that could support a growing patient base, and generate revenue quickly enough to sustain the business — all at the same time.

The Approach

We started with doctors rather than patients. If a physician trusted the model and recommended it, the patient conversation became significantly easier. I focused on building relationships with endocrinologists and general practitioners through clinical demonstrations, one-on-one education sessions and by making it genuinely easy for them to refer patients into our system.

For outreach, I ran OPD-style awareness campaigns that brought our team directly into clinical environments — not to sell, but to educate and build familiarity. This created a steady stream of warm referrals that replaced the need for cold patient acquisition.

On the operational side, I worked closely across vendor coordination, inventory planning and service delivery to make sure that every patient who came through had an experience good enough to generate word-of-mouth. In a healthcare setting, reputation travels fast in both directions.

What I Learned

Building revenue as a founder is fundamentally different from hitting quota as an employee. There's no safety net, no marketing team generating leads, no playbook to follow. Every decision you make — who to partner with, how to price, which customers to prioritise — has a direct and immediate impact on survival.

What worked was staying close to the customer. Not abstractly, but literally — being in the room, listening to objections, watching how doctors and patients interacted with the service and adjusting in real time. Speed of learning was the real competitive advantage.

When you're building from zero, your most valuable asset isn't your product or your pitch. It's how fast you can learn from the market and adapt.

The key takeaway

Selling a new category requires you to sell the problem before the solution. We spent as much energy helping doctors and patients understand why continuous monitoring mattered as we did explaining how our service worked. That sequencing was everything.
