Selling Across Two Continents — What India and North America Taught Me

India · Canada · 2015–2025

The Situation

Over the course of my career I've sold in two very different markets — India and Canada — across industries as varied as genomics, healthcare technology and optical retail. Moving between them wasn't just a change of geography. It was a fundamental reset of almost every assumption I had about how buying decisions get made and how trust gets built.

Most sales training treats "the market" as a monolith. In reality, culture shapes every stage of the sales process — from how you get the first meeting to how you close the deal to what happens after the contract is signed.

What's Different — And Why It Matters

India

Relationship-first culture. Decision makers expect multiple touchpoints before any commercial conversation begins. Hierarchy matters — getting to the right person often requires navigating several layers. Price sensitivity is high and negotiation is expected, sometimes as a sign of respect.

Canada / North America

Process-first culture. Buyers expect structure, clear timelines and documented proposals. Decisions move through formal evaluation frameworks. Price is less negotiable but value justification is scrutinised carefully. Directness is respected — beating around the bush loses credibility fast.

Neither approach is better. They're just different — and the salespeople who struggle most when crossing markets are the ones who try to apply one playbook to both.

What I Had to Unlearn

When I moved to Canada, my instinct was to build rapport slowly before getting to business — the way I'd always worked in India. I quickly realised that Canadian buyers interpreted this as a lack of preparation. They wanted me to get to the point faster, lead with clear value propositions and respect their time explicitly.

The adjustment wasn't about being less relational — it was about expressing that relationship differently. In Canada, reliability and follow-through are the relationship. Showing up on time, delivering what you promised and being straightforward about limitations builds trust faster than extended small talk ever could.

What Transferred Perfectly

The fundamentals of great sales are genuinely universal. Listening deeply, understanding the real problem beneath the stated problem, building genuine credibility and delivering on every promise — these work in Chennai, in Toronto and everywhere in between. Culture changes the packaging. It doesn't change the core.

My scientific background also travelled well. Whether I was talking to a lab director in India or a procurement team in Canada, the ability to engage with their technical reality rather than just their commercial one consistently opened doors that a purely sales-focused approach wouldn't have.

The best salespeople I've met in every market share one trait — they're genuinely curious about the person across the table. That curiosity is culturally universal even when everything else is different.

The key takeaway

Adapt your style aggressively but protect your substance completely. The way you communicate changes by market. What you stand for — integrity, preparation, genuine client focus — should never change at all.
