What Great Sales Emails Look Like vs Bad Ones

February 2026 · 5 min read

The average B2B decision maker receives over 100 emails a day. Most are deleted without being opened. A handful get a skim. Very few get a reply. The difference between the ones that land and the ones that don't isn't luck — it's craft.

I've written thousands of sales emails across my career. I've also been on the receiving end of them. What follows is a side-by-side breakdown of the patterns that separate emails that get responses from emails that get ignored — using real examples of the types of messages I see every day.

Example 1 — The Cold Outreach

✗ What not to do
✓ What works
Subject: Synergise Your Sales Pipeline with Our Award-Winning Platform
To: prospect@company.com
Hi [First Name],

I hope this email finds you well! I wanted to reach out because we help companies like yours 10x their revenue using our AI-powered sales platform.

We've worked with hundreds of companies across many industries and our clients see results within 30 days.

Would you be open to a 30-minute call this week or next?

Best,
Dhaval
Subject: Your expansion into the US market
To: prospect@company.com
Hi Sarah,

I saw your announcement last week about expanding into North America — impressive move, especially given how competitive that market is right now.

I work with B2B sales teams navigating exactly that transition. One thing that consistently trips up India-based teams entering North America is pipeline visibility across time zones — is that something you're currently solving for?

Happy to share what's worked for a few teams in similar positions — 5 minutes on a call or just a reply here works fine.

Dhaval
  • Unfilled placeholder left in
  • Vague, unverifiable claims
  • No research, no personalisation
  • Asks for 30 mins immediately
  • Subject line is pure noise
  • References a real, specific trigger
  • Names the exact problem they likely have
  • Low-friction ask — reply or 5 mins
  • Subject line creates curiosity
  • Feels like a human wrote it

Example 2 — The Follow-Up

✗ What not to do
✓ What works
Subject: Following up
To: prospect@company.com
Hi Sarah,

Just following up on my previous email. I wanted to check if you had a chance to review it?

Please let me know if you're interested and we can schedule a call.

Thanks,
Dhaval
Subject: Quick thought on your US launch
To: prospect@company.com
Hi Sarah,

I came across this piece on why Indian SaaS companies struggle with North American outbound — thought it was relevant given what you're building.

[link]

No pressure on a call — just thought it was worth sharing. If the timing ever works, the door's open.

Dhaval
  • "Just following up" — zero value
  • Puts burden on the prospect
  • Adds nothing new to the conversation
  • Feels desperate, not confident
  • Brings something genuinely useful
  • Removes pressure explicitly
  • Keeps the door open without chasing
  • Builds credibility through generosity

Example 3 — The Re-Engagement

✗ What not to do
✓ What works
Subject: Are you still interested?
To: prospect@company.com
Hi Sarah,

I haven't heard from you in a while and wanted to check in. Are you still looking for a solution?

If priorities have changed, please let me know so I can close out your file.

Thanks,
Dhaval
Subject: Still relevant?
To: prospect@company.com
Hi Sarah,

It's been a little while — I imagine things have been busy on your end.

If the pipeline visibility challenge we spoke about is still on your radar, I'd love to pick it back up. If priorities have shifted, no worries at all — just say the word and I'll check back in Q3.

Either way, good luck with the launch.

Dhaval
  • Passive-aggressive tone
  • "Close out your file" is a manipulation tactic
  • Makes the prospect feel guilty
  • Assumes disinterest, not circumstance
  • Acknowledges their reality with empathy
  • References the specific problem discussed
  • Gives a genuine out with a future date
  • Ends warmly — relationship preserved

The Principles Behind Every Good Sales Email

Looking across all three examples, the same principles show up every time. Good sales emails are specific — they reference something real about the prospect's world. They lead with the prospect's problem, not your product. They ask for something small, not something big. And they feel like they were written by a person who genuinely did their homework, not generated by a template and fired at a list.

Bad sales emails do the opposite. They're generic, self-centred, presumptuous about the prospect's interest and ask for too much too soon. They treat the inbox like a broadcast channel rather than a conversation.

The best sales email you'll ever write is the one that makes the recipient think you wrote it only for them. Because you did.

One test before you hit send

Read your email back and ask: could this have been sent to 500 people with just a name swap? If yes, rewrite it. The moment of personalisation that makes it feel individual is almost always the difference between a reply and a delete.
