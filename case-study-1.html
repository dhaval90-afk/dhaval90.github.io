Case Study · InterpretOmics

Selling Into a Market That Didn't Know It Needed You

InterpretOmics · India · 2015–2017

The Situation

When I joined InterpretOmics as an Account Executive in 2015, genomic interpretation was still a foreign concept to most clinical laboratories and hospital procurement teams in India. The product was genuinely ahead of its time — but that was precisely the problem. You can't sell a solution to someone who doesn't yet believe they have the problem.

There was no existing pipeline, no warm leads, no brand recognition in the market. Just a scientifically compelling product, a blank prospect list and the expectation that I'd build something from nothing.

The Challenge

The buying journey in regulated healthcare environments is uniquely complex. Procurement decisions involve multiple stakeholders — lab directors, clinical heads, procurement officers and sometimes hospital management — each with different priorities and different languages. Add to that the compliance requirements, the long evaluation cycles and the deep scepticism that comes with introducing an unfamiliar technology, and you have one of the hardest selling environments I've encountered.

The biggest obstacle wasn't price or competition. It was awareness. Before I could sell anything, I had to educate. And before I could educate, I had to earn the right to be in the room.

The Approach

I leaned heavily on my scientific background — an MSc in Molecular Medicine — to open doors that a traditional salesperson couldn't. Instead of leading with a product pitch, I led with peer-level conversations about genomics, clinical utility and where the science was heading. Lab directors responded very differently to someone who could discuss methodology than to someone reading from a brochure.

I built my outreach strategy around three channels: cold calling procurement teams to get initial meetings, targeted outreach to clinical lab heads through professional networks, and relationship-building with doctors who could act as internal champions. Each channel required a completely different conversation.

Once in the room, I focused on demonstrating value before asking for anything — sharing relevant research, running informal educational sessions and connecting their existing challenges to what the technology could solve. Trust had to come before the transaction.

What I Learned

Selling into an unaware market is a patience game and an education game. The salespeople who try to shortcut the education phase — jumping straight to the pitch before the prospect understands the problem — lose. The ones who invest in building genuine understanding, even when it feels slow, end up with advocates inside the organisation who sell on their behalf.

The hardest part wasn't closing the deal. It was getting the prospect to see the problem clearly enough to want a solution at all.

The key takeaway

In markets where awareness is low, your first job isn't to sell — it's to teach. Build credibility through knowledge, earn trust through consistency, and the commercial conversation follows naturally.
